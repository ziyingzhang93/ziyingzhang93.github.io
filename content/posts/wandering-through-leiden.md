## A Return Visit

There are cities you visit once and forget, and cities that quietly take root in your memory. Leiden is the second kind.

During my years in the Netherlands studying at Wageningen, I would occasionally take the train to Leiden for no particular reason. The town is small enough to walk in an afternoon, old enough to feel like stepping back in time, and quiet enough to think clearly.

## The Canals

Dutch canals have a stillness that photographs never quite capture. In Leiden, they wind through the old town center like a gentle circulatory system — past the Hortus Botanicus, under bridges draped with bicycles, alongside houses whose front doors open directly onto the water.

On a windless day, the reflections are so crisp that you can't tell which way is up.

## The Bookshops

Leiden has an unreasonable number of secondhand bookshops for a town its size. I found a copy of Watson and Crick's original DNA paper reprinted in a slim volume, tucked between Dutch novels and old atlases. It cost three euros.

There's something about browsing physical books that no algorithm can replicate — the serendipity of reaching for one title and discovering another.

## Why Travel Matters for Research

I've come to believe that the best ideas don't arrive at your desk. They arrive on walks, in unfamiliar streets, in the gap between languages. Travel doesn't distract from research — it creates the mental space where unexpected connections form.

Leiden taught me that. Every time.
