## The Gap

There is a particular kind of loneliness that comes from living between languages. Not the loneliness of being misunderstood, but the subtler feeling of knowing that no single language captures everything you want to say.

I grew up thinking in Chinese. I studied science in English. I dream in a mixture of both. And somewhere in the gap between these two systems lives something that neither can fully express.

## What Gets Lost

Chinese has words for feelings that English doesn't. The concept of yuanfen — a fateful coincidence, a connection that was meant to be — has no clean English equivalent. You can explain it, but the explanation takes a paragraph where a single word would do.

English, on the other hand, gives me precision in science. "Transcriptomic heterogeneity" is crisp and exact. The Chinese equivalent works, but it never feels as compact.

## What Gets Found

Living between languages also creates unexpected gifts. You begin to see the edges of each language — the places where assumptions are built into grammar, where worldviews are encoded in vocabulary. This double vision is disorienting at first, then illuminating.

In bioinformatics, I work with the language of code, the language of biology, and the language of statistics. Each has its own grammar, its own elegance, its own blind spots. Perhaps growing up bilingual prepared me for this kind of translation work — moving between systems of meaning, looking for the signal that persists across all of them.

## A Quiet Advantage

I've stopped seeing my in-between-ness as a limitation. It's a perspective. The ability to stand slightly outside any single framework — linguistic, cultural, disciplinary — and see it with fresh eyes.

The best research, like the best writing, happens at the borders. Between disciplines, between languages, between the familiar and the unknown.
