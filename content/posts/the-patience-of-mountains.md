## Going Up

The Lake District in January is not a place for casual visitors. The sky hangs low, the trails are muddy, and the wind finds every gap in your jacket. I went anyway.

From London, it takes about four hours by train — long enough to read, short enough to feel impulsive. I arrived in Windermere as the last light was fading, checked into a small B&B, and set my alarm for dawn.

## The Walk

The route I chose was simple: Helvellyn via Striding Edge. In summer, it's a popular hike. In winter, the ridge is icy and the path demands attention. Every step matters.

There's a meditative quality to walking in difficult conditions. Your mind empties of everything except the next foothold, the angle of the wind, the texture of rock under your fingers. For someone who spends most of their days staring at screens and data, this kind of embodied focus is rare and precious.

## What Mountains Teach

Mountains operate on geological time. The rock beneath my boots was formed hundreds of millions of years ago. The valleys were carved by glaciers that moved centimeters per year over millennia.

Standing on a summit, you feel this temporal scale in your body. Your research deadline, your paper revision, your code that won't compile — all of it shrinks to the appropriate size.

## Coming Down

The descent is always harder on the knees and easier on the mind. By the time I reached the valley floor, I had a clearer sense of what mattered in my research and what was just noise.

I took the train back to London the next morning, muddy boots and all, and sat down at my desk with more patience than I'd had in months.
