## Two Worlds, One Thread

When I left Janssen R&D to begin a PhD at King's College London, people often asked: why go back to academia after building a career in industry? The answer is simple but took years to crystallize — I wanted to go deeper.

## What Industry Taught Me

Working at Janssen and Genergy Bio-Technology gave me something invaluable: the ability to work fast, communicate across disciplines, and deliver results that matter beyond a publication. In pharma, your analysis doesn't just sit in a paper — it informs whether a drug candidate moves forward or stops.

I learned to integrate messy, real-world data. To build pipelines that others could use. To explain a UMAP plot to a medicinal chemist who had never heard of dimensionality reduction. These skills don't come from textbooks.

## What Drew Me Back

But industry also showed me the limits of applied work under time pressure. I kept encountering questions I couldn't pursue — questions about cancer heterogeneity, about the molecular mechanisms underlying treatment resistance, about the fundamental biology of disease progression.

A PhD offered the time and space to chase those questions properly.

## The Transition

Moving from Shanghai to London, from Mandarin-speaking teams to international ones, from fixed deliverables to open-ended research — the adjustment was real. But the core work remains the same: making sense of biological data, one dataset at a time.

## Advice for Others Considering the Switch

If you're a bioinformatician in industry thinking about a PhD: your experience is an asset, not a detour. You'll bring practical skills, domain knowledge, and a results-oriented mindset that many purely academic researchers lack. The key is finding a research question that genuinely excites you — one worth spending several years of your life on.

For me, that question lives at the intersection of cancer biology and computational science. And I'm just getting started.
